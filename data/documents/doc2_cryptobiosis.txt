The most remarkable feature of tardigrades is their ability to enter a state of suspended animation known as cryptobiosis. When environmental conditions become intolerable—such as extreme desiccation, freezing temperatures, or lack of oxygen—tardigrades can suspend their metabolism almost completely. 

The most common form of cryptobiosis in tardigrades is anhydrobiosis, triggered by a lack of water. During this process, the tardigrade curls into a desiccated, pill-like state called a 'tun'. In this tun state, their metabolism slows to less than 0.01% of normal, and their water content drops to as low as 1%. 

To protect their cellular structures during severe dehydration, they synthesize protective molecules. One significant molecule is trehalose, a special sugar that replaces water inside the cells, preventing the collapse of cellular membranes and the crystallization of remaining water, which would otherwise shred delicate cellular machinery. Other protective mechanisms involve intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs) called Tardigrade-specific intrinsically disordered proteins (TDPs). These proteins form a glass-like matrix inside the cells, locking sensitive proteins and molecules in place to prevent them from denaturing or aggregating until water returns.
