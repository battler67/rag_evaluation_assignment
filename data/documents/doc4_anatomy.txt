A tardigrade's body is roughly cylindrical, typically divided into four body segments along with a distinct head. Each body segment bears a pair of legs equipped with four to eight claws or suction disks, depending on the species. The claws aid in gripping the substrate, allowing them to crawl slowly and deliberately, giving rise to the 'water bear' moniker.

Tardigrades possess a complete digestive system, but they lack distinct circulatory or respiratory systems. Instead of blood, their body cavity is filled with fluid (hemolymph) that transports nutrients and oxygen to cells, and gas exchange occurs directly across their body surface. Because of this diffusion-based respiration, they must remain covered in at least a microscopic layer of water to breathe.

Their nervous system is segmented and ladder-like, similar to that of arthropods and annelidic worms, consisting of a dorsal brain (a lobed neural mass) and a ventral nerve cord with individual ganglia for each body segment. Many species possess light-sensitive eyespots, typically consisting of a single pigment cup cell.
