In their cryptobiotic state, tardigrades exhibit resistance to an astonishing array of extreme conditions. They can survive temperatures as low as -272 °C (-458 °F), which is just a degree above absolute zero, and temperatures as high as 150 °C (300 °F) for several minutes. They can also withstand pressures up to 6,000 atmospheres, which is nearly six times the pressure found in the deepest ocean trenches.

Furthermore, tardigrades are extraordinarily resilient to radiation. They can survive doses of ionizing radiation (such as X-rays and gamma rays) hundreds of times higher than the lethal dose for a human. This resistance is not only active in the tun state but surprisingly maintained even when hydrated and active.

Their hardiness was famously demonstrated in 2007 during the FOTON-M3 European Space Agency mission. Thousands of dehydrated tardigrades were exposed to the vacuum of space, severe cosmic radiation, and intense solar UV radiation for 10 days. Upon returning to Earth and being rehydrated, a significant portion of them revived, and many subsequently reproduced successfully, making them the first known animals to survive direct exposure to the extreme vacuum of space.
