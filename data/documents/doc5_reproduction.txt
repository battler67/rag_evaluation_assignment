Tardigrades employ various reproductive strategies. Some species reproduce sexually, requiring both males and females, while others are parthenogenic, consisting entirely of females capable of laying unfertilized eggs that develop into clones of the mother. Parthenogenesis is particularly advantageous in harsh isolated environments, as a single tardigrade can establish a new colony without the need for a mate.

During reproduction, females typically lay one to thirty eggs at a time. The eggs may be deposited freely in the environment, attached to the substrate, or curiously, left behind in the female’s molted exoskeleton (cuticle) for protection. Development is direct, meaning there is no larval stage; offspring hatch as miniature versions of adults, equipped with the exact number of cells they will have for their entire life. Instead of adding cells to grow, their existing cells simply increase in size, a phenomenon called eutely.

The active lifespan of a tardigrade varies by species but generally ranges from a few months to a maximum of two and a half years. However, this physiological lifespan does not account for time spent in cryptobiosis. A tardigrade that frequently enters the tun state can theoretically stretch out its lifespan over several decades.
