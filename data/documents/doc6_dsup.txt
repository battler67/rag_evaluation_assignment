One of the most intense areas of tardigrade research concerns their resistance to DNA-damaging radiation. While many organisms survive severe radiation by deploying highly efficient DNA repair mechanisms after the damage has occurred (such as the bacterium Deinococcus radiodurans), certain tardigrades utilize a preventative approach.

In 2016, researchers sequencing the genome of the tardigrade species Ramazzottius varieornatus discovered a unique protein, which they named Dsup (Damage suppressor). The Dsup protein is highly abundant in tardigrade cells and binds directly to their DNA. By forming a protective physical shield around the genetic material, Dsup significantly reduces the amount of DNA damage inflicted by hydroxyl radicals, which are generated when ionizing radiation interacts with water molecules in the cell.

Astoundingly, when researchers engineered human cultured cells to express the tardigrade Dsup protein in laboratory experiments, those human cells gained enhanced resistance to X-ray radiation, suffering roughly 40% less DNA damage than normal control cells. This discovery highlights the potential for tardigrade biology to eventually inform medical technologies, such as improving the preservation of biological materials or protecting human cells in radiation therapies or space exploration.
