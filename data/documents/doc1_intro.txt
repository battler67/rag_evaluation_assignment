Tardigrades, commonly known as water bears or moss piglets, are near-microscopic, eight-legged animals belonging to the phylum Tardigrada. First described by German zoologist Johann August Ephraim Goeze in 1773, they are renowned for their incredible resilience and ability to survive in environments that would be fatal to almost all other known forms of life. 

Tardigrades are ubiquitous, found in diverse environments across the globe, from the deep sea to the highest mountain peaks, and from tropical rainforests to the Antarctic ice. They typically live in a film of water surrounding mosses, lichens, and leaf litter. Because they require water to remain active, they are considered aquatic animals, even those living in seemingly terrestrial environments.

They range in size from 0.1 to 1.5 millimeters, making them visible under low-power microscopes. Their diet consists mostly of plant cells, algae, and small invertebrates, which they pierce and suck the contents from using specialized stylets in their mouths. Despite their small size, tardigrades have complex anatomies, with a brain, a well-developed nervous system, a multi-lobed digestive tract, and muscles attached directly to their exoskeleton.
